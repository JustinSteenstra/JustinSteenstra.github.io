WAAM Powder Feeder

(Wire-Arc Additive Manufacturing — Modular Powder Feeder Integration)

Skills

CAD, Mechanical Integration, Reverse Engineering (digital calipers), Resin 3D Printing, Assembly & Fit-Up, Design for Serviceability, Rapid Iteration

Objective

The objective of this project was to integrate a powder feeder onto a WAAM robotic system to enable controlled powder delivery during printing for future material research. The system needed to mount securely to the torch/robot, avoid collisions during motion, and be easy to assemble and adjust during testing.

My Role

  • I was the sole designer of the mounting hardware used to attach the powder feeder to the WAAM torch/robot.
  • I created a full-scale 3D CAD model of the WAAM torch head by measuring the physical torch with digital calipers to ensure correct dimensions and mounting placement.
  • I resin 3D printed the mounting components and assembled the full system onto the robot for testing.

Design Process

I treated the mounting task as a real integration problem: the mount needed to be stiff, repeatable, and safe across the robot’s motion, while also being easy to install and service during lab testing.

  • Defined constraints and success criteria. I started by identifying what could break the integration: torch geometry uncertainty, limited clearance near the torch, vibration from motion, and the risk of interference during robot travel. My design goal was a mount that held position reliably and could be removed or adjusted quickly.
  • Reverse engineered the torch head for accurate CAD interfaces. Because mounting success depends on real geometry, I measured the WAAM torch head with digital calipers and built a full-scale CAD model. This gave me a trusted reference for clamp surfaces, fastener placement, cable clearance, and final mount location.
  • Designed the mount for stiffness and alignment. I designed clamp-style mounting geometry so the feeder could be positioned consistently relative to the torch. I focused on preventing rotation/slip and making sure the assembly stayed aligned after tightening.
  • Designed for serviceability and iteration. I made sure the mount could be installed and removed without redesigning other hardware. This mattered because testing required multiple assembly cycles, tuning, and quick fixes.
  • Fabricated and integrated. I resin 3D printed the final mounting parts, checked fit on the physical torch, and then assembled the powder feeder onto the robot. I validated that everything stayed secure and that the setup was practical for real lab use.

Results

  • The powder feeder was successfully mounted to the WAAM system using my custom resin-printed mount and assembly hardware.
  • The integration supported robot motion testing and practical use without obvious interference issues.
  • The final setup enabled continued testing and iteration by making the feeder assembly removable and adjustable.
